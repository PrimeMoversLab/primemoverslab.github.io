Introduction

Agriculture is the foundation of civilization. No industry touches more lives, and no industry’s success is more critical to our continued flourishing. While modern agriculture is a story of dramatic success, enabling unprecedented increases in food production and population growth, the industry faces the dire challenge of feeding the global population (that is projected to grow to 10 billion by 2050) in the midst of a worsening climate crisis [1]. Agriculture faces the additional challenge that, while it is the industry perhaps most vulnerable to the near-term impacts of climate change, many of its most successful practices contribute to and accelerate climate change.

Fortunately, innovators at the forefront of science are innovating new solutions to address the most urgent needs in agriculture. Because Prime Movers Lab’s core goal is investing in breakthrough scientific startups with the potential to improve billions of lives, focusing on the opportunities that exist in agricultural technology (agtech) is a natural fit. This thought paper begins with a high-level overview of the state of agriculture today and the problems it faces, then breaks down the emerging solutions to those problems by industry sector and explains our investment theses in those areas.

From Fertile Crescent to Double-Edged Sword: Agriculture’s Massive Success Sows Trouble

After thousands of years of slow, steady development in agriculture (punctuated by breakthrough periods such as the 8-13th century Arab Agricultural Revolution and the 18th century British Agricultural Revolution), the 20th century saw an agtech revolution that radically increased production and transformed the methods of food production and distribution. This so-called Green Revolution involved the mechanization of farm labor, the development of high-yield crops, improvements in irrigation practices, and the introduction of synthetic fertilizers and pesticides. These innovations exponentially increased agricultural productivity and enabled the abundance most people enjoy today.

Sustaining the world’s current population would be impossible without the technologies of the Green Revolution. Advances in plant genetics in the mid-20th century enabled the American agronomist (and Nobel Peace Prize laureate) Norman Borlaug’s to develop high-yield, disease resistant wheat varieties credited with saving more than a billion people from starvation. Chinese agronomist Yuan Longping’s 1970’s development of the world’s first hybrid rice varieties saved an additional billion lives [citation]. Without synthetic nitrogen fertilizers enabled by the chemistry of the Haber-Bosch process, it is estimated that 30-50% of the world’s crops would fail [citation]. Productivity increases from mechanized farming equipment have transformed the labor force, enabling modern industrialization; for example, whereas more than 50% of humans earned their living in agriculture throughout most of human history, today currently only ~1% of US workers are employed in agriculture [citation]. Thus, as much as any technological breakthrough, agtech enabled and sustains the modern world.

Source: I. Welfeld, Where We Live (Simon and Schuster)

Unfortunately, the rapid implementation of these advances has resulted in severe unintended consequences. Along with increased yields, agricultural industrialization also intensified environmental pollution from chemical inputs, exacerbated soil erosion due to aggressive farming practices, amplified social inequalities by excluding small-scale farmers, and resulted in unsustainable levels of greenhouse gas emissions. All told, modern industrial agriculture contributes 21-37% of total net anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions (when including emissions from agriculture itself, land use change, and pre/post production activities) [citation]. It isn’t news to anyone that our current global warming trajectory, if continued unchecked, will result in catastrophic, existential consequences, highlighting the urgent need for more sustainable agricultural practices [citation].

Modern agriculture is uniquely situated as an industry that relies on stable climate and weather conditions while simultaneously contributing to climate destabilization through greenhouse gas emissions and land-use changes. Inherent inefficiencies in the application of synthetic fertilizers lead to 80% of human-caused nitrous oxide emissions, a greenhouse gas 265 times more potent than CO2 [citation]. Climate change impacts on land can also exacerbate emissions, for example through increased wildfires, permafrost thaw, and soil erosion. In turn, the accelerated global warming resulting from such emissions drives an increase in extreme weather events such as drought, flood, and wildfire that threaten agricultural productivity, leading to a need for more global-warming-causing agricultural inputs to offset losses [citation]. Meanwhile, food production needs to increase by as much as 68% by 2050 to feed our growing global population, again calling for an increased application of climate change-inducing (and therefore yields-threatening) ag inputs [citation].
